There is a famous saying: *"The word is not the thing."*

The word "Water" is not the liquid that quenches your thirst.

The word "Love" is not the feeling in your chest.

This sounds obvious, but Krishnamurti points out that we spend almost our entire lives living in the **Word** (the Image) rather than the **Reality** (the Thing).

We move through the world with a thick screen of labels, judgments, and memories between us and life. We think we are seeing the world, but we are actually just looking at our own mental projections.

## 1. The Safety Mechanism

Why does the mind do this? Why can't we just see things directly?

It is a shortcut. The brain is an efficiency machine.

If you had to look at a car with fresh eyes every time you saw one, you would be overwhelmed. So the brain says, *"That is a car. I know what that is. Ignore it."*

This is useful for survival.

But it is disastrous for living.

We apply this same efficiency to:

- **People:** "He is an angry person." (Now I don't need to look at him; I have labeled him).

- **Groups:** "They are Liberals/Conservatives." (Now I don't need to listen to them; I know what they think).

- **Nature:** "That is a tree." (I have named it, so I stop looking at it).

Once we label something, we stop being in contact with it. We put it in a box and move on.

## 2. Eating the Menu

Imagine going to a restaurant, reading the menu, and then eating the paper menu instead of the food.

That is how we live psychologically.

- We pursue the *idea* of success, not the work itself.

- We love the *image* of our partner, not the human being.

- We fear the *concept* of death, not the reality of it.

Krishnamurti asks us to notice this gap. The image is always old; it is made of the past. Life is always new; it is happening now. As long as the image exists, we can never touch the new.

## 3. The Source of Prejudice

This machinery of image-making is the root of all prejudice.

When you look at a stranger, do you see a human being?

Or do you instantly see:

- Their skin color?

- Their clothes (Rich/Poor)?

- Their gender?

Before they have even spoken, your mind has projected a dozen images onto them. You are not interacting with them; you are interacting with your culture's programming.

> "To look without the image is to look without the background of your conditioning."

## 4. The Art of Direct Perception

So, how do we clean the lens?

We don't try to destroy the images violently. We just **notice the naming process.**

Try this:

Look at a flower, a cloud, or a tree.

Your mind will instantly say: *"That is a Rose."*

Notice that word. Notice how, the moment you named it "Rose," your curiosity stopped. You classified it and filed it away.

Now, try to look at it **without the word**.

Look at the color, the texture, the fragility of it, without calling it anything.

Suddenly, the flower becomes vibrant. It becomes unique. You are actually seeing *this* flower, not the generic concept of a flower.

## 5. Daily Experiment: The Nameless Walk

**Time Required:** 5 minutes.

**Location:** Anywhere (a park, your office, a busy street).

1. **Walk slowly.**

2. **Look around.**

3. **The Challenge:** Try to observe things without naming them mentally.

   - See the movement of the leaves, but don't say "Tree."

   - See the person walking, but don't say "Man" or "Woman."

   - Hear the noise, but don't say "Car."

You will find it difficult. The mind *wants* to label.

But in the gaps between the labels, you might feel a sudden rush of aliveness. You are taking the filter off the camera lens.

## Closing Thoughts

Living without images is dangerous—not physically, but psychologically. It means you are vulnerable. You cannot hide behind your opinions anymore.

But it is the only way to be truly awake.

Now that we understand how we project images onto the world, we can see how this affects the most important area of all: **Relationships.**

How do these images destroy our connection with the people we love?

We explored this in **Part 5: Relationships**, and now with this understanding of images, you can see even more clearly why we relate to pictures instead of people.
